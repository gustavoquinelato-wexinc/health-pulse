# McKinsey Innovation Horizons Framework

## üåü Overview

The Innovation Horizons framework, developed by McKinsey & Company, is a strategic approach to portfolio management that helps organizations balance their innovation investments across three time horizons. This framework enables companies to maintain current business performance while developing future growth opportunities.

## üéØ The Three Horizons

### üîß Horizon 1 (H1): Core Business Optimization

- **Focus**: Extending and defending core businesses
- **Timeframe**: Immediate to short-term (0-1 years)
- **Risk Level**: Low
- **Expected Returns**: Predictable, immediate
- **Ideal Portfolio Allocation**: ~70%

#### Characteristics:
- Incremental improvements to existing products and services
- Process optimizations and efficiency gains
- Cost reduction initiatives
- Maintaining market share in established markets

#### Examples:
- Feature enhancements to existing products
- UI/UX improvements
- Performance optimizations
- Compliance and regulatory updates
- Technical debt reduction

---

### üöÄ Horizon 2 (H2): Emerging Opportunities

- **Focus**: Building emerging businesses and opportunities
- **Timeframe**: Medium-term (1-3 years)
- **Risk Level**: Moderate
- **Expected Returns**: Growth within 1-3 years
- **Ideal Portfolio Allocation**: ~20%

#### Characteristics:
- Extensions to adjacent markets or customer segments
- New capabilities that build on existing strengths
- Emerging business models with proven potential
- Strategic partnerships and acquisitions

#### Examples:
- New product lines that leverage existing capabilities
- Expansion into adjacent markets
- Integration of emerging technologies
- New delivery or business models
- Strategic partnerships

---

### üî¨ Horizon 3 (H3): Disruptive Innovations

- **Focus**: Creating viable options for future business
- **Timeframe**: Long-term (3+ years)
- **Risk Level**: High
- **Expected Returns**: Potentially transformative but uncertain
- **Ideal Portfolio Allocation**: ~10%

#### Characteristics:
- Disruptive innovations and breakthrough technologies
- Research and development of new capabilities
- Exploration of entirely new markets or business models
- Ventures and experiments with high uncertainty

#### Examples:
- Research into emerging technologies
- Radical new business models
- Disruptive product concepts
- Exploratory market research
- Strategic experiments and pilots

## üìä Innovation Index Scoring Guidelines (1-10 Scale)

### üü¢ Low Innovation (1-3) - Typically H1 Initiatives

- **Score 1**: Routine maintenance or minor bug fixes with no functional changes
- **Score 2**: Small incremental improvements to existing features
- **Score 3**: Moderate enhancements that improve existing functionality without changing core behavior

### üü° Medium Innovation (4-7) - Typically H2 Initiatives

- **Score 4**: Significant enhancements that extend existing capabilities
- **Score 5**: New features that build upon existing product architecture
- **Score 6**: Adaptations of existing solutions to new markets or user segments
- **Score 7**: Substantial new capabilities that require moderate technical changes

### üî¥ High Innovation (8-10) - Typically H3 Initiatives

- **Score 8**: Major new capabilities requiring significant technical changes
- **Score 9**: Novel solutions that create new market opportunities or business models
- **Score 10**: Breakthrough innovations that fundamentally transform the product or market

### üîç Scoring Factors to Consider

1. **Novelty**: How new is this idea to the market?
2. **Disruption Potential**: How much could this change existing business models?
3. **Technical Challenge**: How technically difficult is the implementation?
4. **Market Impact**: How significantly could this affect market dynamics?
5. **Organizational Change**: How much would this change internal processes or capabilities?

## ‚öñÔ∏è Portfolio Balance Principles

### üìà Optimal Distribution

- **70/20/10 Rule**: As a general guideline, organizations should allocate approximately 70% of their innovation resources to H1 initiatives, 20% to H2, and 10% to H3
- **Industry Variation**: The optimal distribution may vary by industry, company maturity, and market conditions

### üéØ Key Considerations for Portfolio Balance

1. **Strategic Alignment**: All initiatives across horizons should align with the organization's strategic vision
2. **Resource Allocation**: Ensure appropriate funding, talent, and time allocation across horizons
3. **Risk Management**: Balance risk across the portfolio to ensure sustainability
4. **Transition Planning**: Plan for successful initiatives to move from H3 to H2 to H1 over time
5. **Governance**: Establish different governance and success metrics for each horizon

## üìã Framework Application Guidelines

### For Strategic Planning:
1. **Assess Current Portfolio**: Evaluate existing initiatives across the three horizons
2. **Identify Gaps**: Determine where additional investment is needed
3. **Balance Resources**: Allocate resources according to the 70/20/10 principle
4. **Set Success Metrics**: Define appropriate KPIs for each horizon
5. **Regular Review**: Continuously monitor and adjust portfolio balance

### For Innovation Assessment:
1. **Categorize Initiatives**: Classify each innovation by horizon
2. **Score Innovation Level**: Use the 1-10 scale to assess innovation intensity
3. **Evaluate Strategic Fit**: Ensure alignment with organizational goals
4. **Assess Resource Requirements**: Understand investment needs across horizons
5. **Plan Transition Paths**: Define how initiatives will evolve between horizons

This framework provides a structured approach to innovation portfolio management, ensuring organizations maintain operational excellence while investing in future growth opportunities.
